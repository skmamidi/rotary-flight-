Rotary vs Fixed-Wing

Two ways to fly. One big difference in how lift is made.

Core ⏱ 5 min ○ Not completed
🎯
Why this matters

Science Olympiad tests often ask you to compare how lift is generated. Knowing the difference between rotary and fixed-wing flight helps you answer comparison questions with confidence.

What Both Have in Common

Both fixed-wing airplanes and rotary-wing aircraft need air moving over a shaped surface to create lift. The shape is called an airfoil. The difference is how they get the air to move.

How Fixed Wings Make Lift

In an airplane, the whole aircraft moves forward. Air flows over the fixed wing, creating lower pressure on top and lift.

  • Wing stays in place
  • Aircraft must move forward
  • Needs a runway
  • Cannot hover

How Rotors Make Lift

In a helicopter, the blades spin while the aircraft stays mostly still. The spinning blades move through the air and create lift.

  • Blades spin in a circle
  • Lift comes from rotation
  • No runway needed
  • Can hover and fly backwards

Olympiad Edge

Both create lift using moving air over an airfoil. The motion source differs. For airplanes, the whole vehicle moves. For helicopters, the blades move while the body can stay still. Tests often trap students who think helicopters "defy physics" rather than simply using a different mechanism.

Key Vocabulary

Fixed-wing
Aircraft with rigid wings requiring forward motion for lift.
Rotor
Spinning blades that generate lift for rotary-wing aircraft.
Airfoil
A shaped surface designed to produce lift from airflow.
VTOL
Vertical Takeoff and Landing.
