Lift in Rotary Flight

How spinning blades pull an aircraft upward.

Core ⏱ 5 min ○ Not completed
🎯
Why this matters

Lift is the most important force in flight. If you understand how a rotor blade makes lift, you unlock the secret to hovering, climbing, and autorotation.

What Lift Means

Lift is the upward force that opposes weight. In rotary flight, lift comes from air moving over each rotor blade. Each blade is shaped like a thin wing—curved on top and flatter on the bottom.

As the blade spins, air flows faster over the curved top surface than the bottom. This creates lower pressure on top, pulling the blade upward. Multiply that by all the blades spinning together, and you get enough lift to raise the entire aircraft.

Rotor Blade as a Wing

A rotor blade acts like a wing, but with one key difference: it spins in a circle. This means the blade is constantly moving through air, even when the helicopter itself is not moving forward. That is why helicopters can hover.

💡
Fun Fact

A helicopter rotor tip can move faster than the speed of sound if the rotor spins too fast! Pilots avoid this by controlling rotor speed carefully.

Olympiad Edge

Lift is not "magic upward force." It comes from air movement and blade shape/pitch. Tests may ask: "What causes lift on a rotor blade?" The correct answer involves airspeed over a curved surface and angle of attack—not engine power alone.

Key Vocabulary

Lift
Upward force from air moving over a blade.
Rotor blade
A spinning wing that creates lift.
Airfoil
A shaped surface that produces lift from airflow.
Angle of attack
The angle between the blade and the oncoming air.
