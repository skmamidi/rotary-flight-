Anti-Torque Systems

How helicopters stop themselves from spinning out of control.

Core ⏱ 5 min ○ Not completed
🎯
Why this matters

Without an anti-torque system, a helicopter would spin uncontrollably. Knowing the different solutions helps you answer aircraft-type comparison questions.

Tail Rotor

The most common solution. A small rotor on the tail pushes air sideways, creating a force that opposes main rotor torque. It also lets the pilot control yaw (nose left/right).

Coaxial Solution

Two rotors stacked on the same axis spin in opposite directions. Their torques cancel each other out. No tail rotor needed! This design is compact and safer near obstacles.

Tandem Solution

Two large rotors, one at the front and one at the rear, spin in opposite directions. Their torques cancel. This design is used for heavy-lift helicopters like the CH-47 Chinook.

Olympiad Edge

Single-rotor helicopters need a tail rotor (or NOTAR). Tandem and coaxial designs do not. If a test shows an aircraft with no tail rotor, ask: are there two main rotors? If yes, torque is canceled internally.

Key Vocabulary

Tail rotor
Small rotor that counters torque and controls yaw.
Coaxial
Stacked counter-rotating rotors that cancel torque.
Tandem rotor
Front and rear rotors spinning opposite directions.
